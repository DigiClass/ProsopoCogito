What kind of documents can I upload?

Presently, Recogito supports:

  • UTF-8 plaintext files
  • Images in JPEG, TIFF and PNG format
  • Images served via IIIF (currently, single images only)

We are working towards support for TEI/XML-encoded texts, data tables in CSV format, and support for IIIF collection manifests.

Can I upload unlimited documents?

No. The standard private work spaces have an upload limit of 200 MB. Please get in touch with us if you require additional space.

Are there legal considerations to using Recogito?

UK Copyright Law permits the use of third party material for "private and research study purposes". Uploaded documents are hosted in your private area, and will be accessible only to you - unless you choose to share them with other users. You can make documents publicly available by ticking the "Visible to all" option in your document sharing options. But may strictly only do so in circumstances where the document is not under copyright. (Please also refer to our Terms and Conditions.)

Copyright is an automatic right. But other licensing arrangements may also apply and you must take legal responsibility for adhering to them. In particular, be aware that some "open" Creative Commons licenses have a "No Derivatives" clause which according to most copyright laws includes annotations. You may not make such works public within Recogito without prior consent from the copyright owner.

Can I work on the same document with other people at the same time?

Yes, you can work concurrently on the same document. But at present you will not be able to see other people's edits in real-time like, say, in Google Drive.

What is a gazetteer?

To paraphrase from the introductory chapters of Berman, Mostern & Southall, Placing Names: Enriching and Integrating Gazetteers: Most simply, a gazetteer is a list of places. A digital gazetteer, in particular, will often include names, coordinates and sometimes features types for each place as well. Today, gazetteers are the basis for much of the spatial search and visualization we all have come to take for granted on the Web.

Which gazetteers are currently available in Recogito?

The following gazetteers are currently available

  • Pleiades Gazetteer of the Ancient World
  • Places from the Digital Atlas of the Roman Empire
  • GeoNames

How do I know which gazetteer to use?

Different gazetteers will provide different, often complementary (and sometimes even conflicting), information about places for particular time periods or regions. We recommend that you select the gazetteer you feel best reflects the document that you are working on, e.g. Pleiades for Ancient history, GeoNames for contemporary material.

We aim to add further gazetteers to Recogito over time. In additiona, we are working actively with the gazetteer community to assist in the alignment of gazetteers - which means that place references you associate with one gazetteer will automatically become associated with relevant places in other gazetteers as well.

What is Linked Data and how does Recogito use it?

Linked Data is a way of connecting online resources that have some element in common. In our case, that common element is Place: Recogito connects documents to online gazetteers and makes annotations available to you in a Linked Data format called the Open Annotation Data Model.

How can I cite Recogito in a bibliography?

Please list our URL and the month and year you accessed it, as in the following example:

Recogito, an initiative of Pelagios Commons: http://recogito.pelagios.org/ (accessed 12 September 2016)
